# Dizziness

## Types of Dizziness

**Lightheadedness** — feeling like you might faint. The room doesn't spin, but you feel unsteady or woozy. Often happens when you stand up too quickly. Usually caused by a temporary drop in blood pressure.

**Vertigo** — a spinning sensation where it feels like you or the room is moving. Often caused by inner ear problems. Can come with nausea and difficulty balancing.

**Disequilibrium** — feeling off-balance or unsteady when walking, without a spinning sensation. More common in older adults and can be related to muscle weakness, joint problems, or medications.

## Common Causes

**Dehydration** — not drinking enough water, especially in hot weather or after exercise. One of the most common and easily fixable causes.

**Low blood pressure** — blood pressure drops when you stand up quickly (called postural hypotension). Common in older adults and people on certain medications.

**Inner ear problems** — conditions like BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo) cause brief episodes of vertigo triggered by head movements. Labyrinthitis and vestibular neuritis cause longer episodes and are usually from viral infections.

**Low blood sugar** — can happen if you skip meals or have diabetes. Comes with shakiness, sweating, and feeling weak.

**Anaemia** — low iron levels mean less oxygen reaches your brain. Causes dizziness along with tiredness, pale skin, and shortness of breath.

**Anxiety** — stress and anxiety can make you feel lightheaded, especially if you're breathing too fast (hyperventilation).

## Self-Care

Sit or lie down immediately when you feel dizzy. Drink water — dehydration is often the culprit. Stand up slowly from sitting or lying down. Avoid sudden head movements if you have vertigo. Eat regular meals to keep blood sugar stable.

## When to See a Doctor

See a GP if dizziness keeps coming back, lasts for more than a few days, or affects your daily activities. Seek urgent help if dizziness comes with chest pain, difficulty breathing, sudden severe headache, weakness or numbness on one side of the body, double vision, difficulty speaking, or loss of consciousness. These could be signs of a stroke or heart problem.
