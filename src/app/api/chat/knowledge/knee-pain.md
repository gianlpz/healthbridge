# Knee Pain

## Common Causes

**Runner's knee (patellofemoral pain)** — a dull ache around or behind the kneecap. Common in runners, cyclists, and people who sit for long periods. Gets worse when going up or down stairs, squatting, or sitting with bent knees for a long time.

**Meniscus tear** — the meniscus is a piece of cartilage that cushions your knee joint. A tear causes pain, swelling, and sometimes a locking or catching feeling in the knee. Often happens during twisting movements in sports.

**Ligament injury (ACL/MCL)** — sudden pain and swelling after a twisting injury, often during sports. You might hear a pop and the knee may feel unstable or give way.

**Osteoarthritis** — wear and tear of the knee joint over time. Causes stiffness (especially in the morning), pain that gets worse with activity, and sometimes swelling. More common in people over 50.

**Tendonitis** — inflammation of a tendon, usually the patellar tendon below the kneecap. Common in people who do a lot of jumping or running. Causes pain just below the kneecap that's worse during activity.

**Bursitis** — inflammation of the fluid-filled sacs (bursae) that cushion the knee. Causes swelling and tenderness. Can be caused by repetitive kneeling.

## Self-Care

Rest the knee and avoid activities that make it worse. Apply ice for 15-20 minutes several times a day to reduce swelling. Use a compression bandage for support. Elevate your leg when resting. Over-the-counter anti-inflammatories like ibuprofen can help with pain and swelling. Gentle strengthening exercises for the muscles around the knee (quadriceps and hamstrings) can help long-term.

## When to Seek Help

See a GP or physiotherapist if pain lasts more than a few weeks, the knee swells up significantly, you can't put weight on it, the knee locks or gives way, or you heard a pop during an injury. Seek urgent care if the knee is very swollen and hot (could be an infection), or if you can't move the knee at all after an injury.
